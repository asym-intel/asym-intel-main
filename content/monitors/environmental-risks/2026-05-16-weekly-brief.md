## Lead Signal

Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation risk intensified this cycle as multiple Tier 2 studies converged on new early warning indicators and quantified carbon cycle feedbacks. A 2026 Communications Earth and Environment paper identified a Gulf Stream abrupt northward displacement of 219 km within two years near Cape Hatteras as an early warning indicator that appears decades before simulated Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation collapse. Satellite altimetry data from 1993 to 2024 and subsurface temperature observations from 1965 to 2024 confirm a statistically significant northward Gulf Stream trend at Cape Hatteras, providing observational grounding for this precursor signal. Within the tipping point tracker, Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation is now characterised as approaching with high preliminary probability, with observational constraints projecting approximately 50 percent weakening by 2100 under a medium emissions scenario that is around 60 percent stronger than model only estimates.

The planetary boundary framing emphasises how these Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation dynamics interact with the climate change boundary. The climate change boundary remains beyond high risk, with the Copernicus April 2026 bulletin confirming the second highest extra polar sea surface temperatures on record and a strengthening El Nino signal in which more than 50 percent of ensemble members exceed 2.5 degrees C amplitude in the Nino3.4 index. A separate Communications Earth and Environment study quantifies an Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation collapse carbon cycle feedback of 47 to 83 ppm additional atmospheric CO2, adding approximately 0.2 degrees C global warming at higher background concentrations. This feedback creates a positive loop between the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation tipping element and the climate change planetary boundary, materially altering long run physical risk envelopes for fossil and coastal assets and reinforcing the current assessment that Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation tipping is the dominant Earth system signal in this reporting cycle.

## Other Developments

**Planetary boundaries status and El Nino linked stressors** The planetary status snapshot records six planetary boundaries exceeded, one at high risk, two in increasing risk, and none in the safe zone, with the aggregate status delta assessed as stable over the week. Within this frame, the climate change boundary remains beyond high risk, anchored in the Copernicus April 2026 bulletin that reports global mean surface temperature anomalies 0.52 degrees C above the 1991 to 2020 average for April 2026 and second highest extra polar sea surface temperatures on record. El Nino development continues to strengthen, with more than 50 percent of ensemble members exceeding 2.5 degrees C amplitude in the Nino3.4 index in the latest seasonal forecast. This El Nino trajectory acts as a cross cutting stressor for biosphere integrity, land system change, freshwater use, and ocean acidification, opening a bleaching window for coral reefs and elevating drought precursors in Central America, Southeast Asia, and northern South America.

**Biosphere integrity and Amazon dieback precursor signals** Biosphere integrity remains beyond high risk, with the Living Planet Index baseline continuing to frame long run biodiversity loss. This cycle did not add new quantified loss data but El Nino development elevates stress on tropical forest biomes and strengthens drought precursor signals relevant to Amazon dieback. In the tipping point tracker, Amazon dieback is assessed as approaching with medium preliminary probability, with an El Nino drought forecast for northern South America identified as the key indicator. The cascade chain links drier conditions to elevated drought, increased fire risk in Amazon transition zones, and downstream food stress and displacement in the Venezuela and Colombia border region, although no new fire or deforestation metrics from national monitoring agencies were added this cycle. The gaps register notes that Inpe Amazon fire data or deforestation metrics would be required to upgrade trajectory assessment confidence.

**Coral reef collapse, aerosol pathways, and attribution gaps** Coral reef collapse is assessed as imminent with high preliminary probability, driven by combined sea surface temperature anomalies and regulatory driven aerosol changes. The tipping point tracker highlights C3S El Nino development and the second warmest March sea surface temperatures as opening a bleaching window, while a Communications Earth and Environment study finds that IMO 2020 ship sulfur cuts reduced aerosol cooling over the Great Barrier Reef, adding 11 watts per square meter radiation and increasing thermal stress by 5 to 10 percent. This aerosol pathway is mirrored in the atmospheric aerosols and novel entities planetary boundaries, where IMO 2020 sulfur cuts are described as reducing aerosol cooling and thereby increasing Great Barrier Reef thermal stress by 5 to 10 percent via a novel pathway, creating unintended ecological consequences while contributing to a wider synthetic chemical and pollutant burden. The attribution gap cases catalogue this as an atmospheric aerosol loading case with no binding framework, and a coral reef bleaching case where there is no international liability framework despite a 5 to 10 percent increase in Great Barrier Reef thermal stress from ship sulfur cuts. The aerosol dual risk flag for the Great Barrier Reef reinforces that 11 watts per square meter added radiation and associated 5 to 10 percent thermal stress increase arise from a regulatory decision that reduces aerosols but imposes regional ecological cost.

**Freshwater, land, and climate security cascades under El Nino** Freshwater use and atmospheric aerosols remain in increasing risk status with stable deltas, while land system change, ocean acidification, and novel entities remain beyond high risk with stable deltas. ## Outlook

Near term monitoring will focus on whether the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation precursor indicators and El Nino trajectory continue to strengthen. The gaps register highlights that freshwater flux readings from the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation Field of View of Systems array at 34 degrees south would allow an upgrade of Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation proximity assessment from assessed to high confidence, and that Inpe Amazon fire and deforestation data would similarly refine Amazon dieback assessments. In water coral bleaching confirmation from NOAA Coral Reef Watch or the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park Authority would allow an upgrade of coral reef collapse proximity from high to confirmed confidence, closing an important observational gap in the current tipping point analysis.

On the socio ecological side, the active cascade chains in the Central America Dry Corridor, Southeast Asia, and northern South America will remain key watchpoints for conflict escalation, migration, and governance stress in the coming months. The absence of progress in loss and damage financing, zero environmental governance composite scores, and unresolved attribution gaps for aerosols, synthetic chemicals, coral reef bleaching, and deep sea mining mean that escalation in physical climate and tipping point signals is not being matched by governance response. Unless forthcoming cycles record movement on these governance and finance fronts, the structural imbalance between accelerating Earth system risks and institutional capacity to manage them will persist, amplifying the implications of any further shifts in Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation, El Nino, or associated tipping systems.
