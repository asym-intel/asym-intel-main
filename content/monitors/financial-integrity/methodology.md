## Overview

The Financial Integrity Monitor (FIM) tracks illicit financial flows, sanctions-evasion architecture, beneficial-ownership opacity, kleptocratic asset networks, and enabler-jurisdiction dynamics. Its defining analytical principle is **architecture over incident**: a structural enabler-jurisdiction assessment is more analytically significant than any individual enforcement action.

The monitor produces strategic intelligence on the infrastructure of illicit finance — the jurisdictions, professional networks, corporate structures, and regulatory gaps that allow illicit financial flows to function at scale. An OFAC designation is a data point; the sanctions-evasion architecture that made the designated activity possible is the product.

The FIM operates as a spoke in the [Asymmetric Intelligence hub-and-spoke architecture](https://asym-intel.info). It receives conflict-theatre context from the [Strategic Conflict & Escalation Monitor](https://asym-intel.info/monitors/conflict-escalation/), state-capture signals from the [World Democracy Monitor](https://asym-intel.info/monitors/democratic-integrity/dashboard.html), and commodity-flow data from the [Global Environmental Risks Monitor](https://asym-intel.info/monitors/environmental-risks/dashboard.html). It feeds financial-integrity signals to all companion monitors where illicit finance intersects their analytical domain.

## 01 · What This Monitor Tracks

The monitor covers five analytical domains, each representing a structural dimension of the global illicit-finance architecture:

**D1 — Sanctions Architecture and Evasion.** Sanctions-regime design, implementation, enforcement, and evasion infrastructure. Standing coverage includes Russian sanctions-evasion networks, Chinese sanctions-circumvention facilitation, Iranian evasion infrastructure, DPRK revenue generation, and EU/US/UK sanctions-regime divergence.

**D2 — Beneficial Ownership and Corporate Transparency.** Beneficial-ownership registry implementation, corporate-transparency legislation, and the use of opaque structures to conceal ownership. Standing coverage includes the EU AML Package, UK beneficial-ownership register effectiveness, US Corporate Transparency Act, and FATF Recommendation 24/25 compliance trajectories.

**D3 — Enabler Jurisdictions and Professional Facilitators.** The role of professional intermediaries and permissive jurisdictions in enabling illicit flows. Standing coverage includes the UK enabler ecosystem, Dubai/UAE financial-centre dynamics, Singapore wealth-management growth, and Swiss banking reform trajectory.

**D4 — Conflict Finance and Extractive-Industry Integrity.** Financial flows sustaining armed conflict and corruption in extractive industries. Standing coverage includes Russian war-economy financing, Sahel conflict-mineral trade, DRC mining-sector governance, and oil-revenue corruption in producer states.

**D5 — Crypto, Digital Assets, and Financial Innovation.** Where digital assets and financial technology create new channels for illicit finance or new tools for transparency and enforcement. Standing coverage includes crypto-facilitated sanctions evasion, DeFi and mixing-service enforcement, stablecoin regulatory frameworks, and CBDC integrity implications.

## 02 · The Analytical Framework

### Architecture Over Incident

Individual enforcement actions and money-laundering cases are symptoms. The FIM tracks the enabling architecture — the jurisdictions, professional networks, corporate structures, and regulatory gaps that allow illicit financial flows to function at scale.

When an enforcement action occurs, the analytical question is not "what was prosecuted?" but "what does this prosecution reveal about the architecture that remains in place?" An OFAC designation against shell companies in a specific jurisdiction triggers a reassessment of that jurisdiction's structural role in the broader evasion architecture — not merely a record of the designation itself.

### Five Standing Trackers

The monitor maintains five standing trackers that carry forward week to week, updated when material evidence warrants — not because a week has passed:

1. **Russian Sanctions-Evasion Architecture** — dark-fleet operations, technology-procurement networks, financial intermediaries, commodity-trade rerouting, and facilitation nodes
2. **EU AML Package Implementation** — AMLA establishment, directive transposition, beneficial-ownership registry interconnection, and the gap between legislative ambition and regulatory reality
3. **FATF Grey List** — current grey-listed jurisdictions, mutual evaluation outcomes, and compliance trajectories assessed for strategic significance
4. **Beneficial-Ownership Register Status** — global registry implementation and effectiveness, tracked through evaluations, assessments, and investigative ground truth
5. **Crypto and Digital-Asset Integrity** — crypto-facilitated sanctions evasion, DeFi enforcement, MiCA implementation, and stablecoin regulatory frameworks

## 03 · Analytical Filters

Four analytical filters are applied to every finding to ensure structural depth and cross-domain awareness:

**F1 — State Capture Filter.** Triggered when the distinction between state and private criminal interest has collapsed — where state institutions protect illicit financial flows rather than prevent them. Assesses whether the state is directing the financial architecture or has been captured by it. Findings with a state-capture dimension are cross-referenced with the World Democracy Monitor.

**F2 — Sanctions Architecture Filter.** Triggered by any documented sanctions-evasion scheme. Requires three-level analysis: the scheme itself, the enabling architecture (jurisdictions, intermediaries, structures), and the strategic consequence (what the evasion achieves — sustaining conflict, preserving kleptocratic wealth, maintaining technology access, or funding proliferation).

**F3 — Enabler Jurisdiction Filter.** Triggered when a jurisdiction's legal or regulatory framework facilitates illicit financial flows. Assesses legal framework design, enforcement reality, the distinction between capacity deficit and political choice, and systemic significance. The absence of enforcement action in an enabler jurisdiction is itself a signal — this filter actively searches for non-enforcement.

**F4 — Conflict Finance Filter.** Triggered by any financial flow documented as funding, sustaining, or profiting from armed conflict. Traces source, channel, and deployment. Cross-referenced with the Strategic Conflict & Escalation Monitor for conflict context and the Environmental Risks Monitor for commodity-flow data.

## 04 · Source Standards

Sources are assessed on a four-tier hierarchy:

**Tier 1 — Institutional and Regulatory.** FATF mutual evaluations, OFAC/OFSI/EU sanctions designations, FinCEN advisories, national regulator enforcement actions, UNODC assessments. Always cited first; always linked to primary documents.

**Tier 2 — Investigative and Forensic.** OCCRP, ICIJ, Global Witness, Bellingcat, Finance Uncovered, Forbidden Stories, and established cross-border financial investigation outlets. Primary source for identifying illicit-finance architecture ahead of regulatory acknowledgement. The gap between Tier 2 findings and Tier 1 enforcement is itself a signal.

**Tier 3 — Quality Journalism and Data.** Financial Times, Bloomberg, Reuters, Wall Street Journal for financial-crime and sanctions reporting. Kpler/Windward for dark-fleet and commodity-flow tracking. Used for timeline, enforcement-response context, and market-impact framing.

**Tier 4 — Think Tanks and Academic.** RUSI, Chatham House, Carnegie, Transparency International, Global Financial Integrity, Basel Institute on Governance, Tax Justice Network. Used for structural framing and policy analysis.

## 05 · Confidence Framework

Each finding carries a confidence label reflecting the evidentiary standard met:

| Level | Standard |
|---|---|
| **Confirmed** | Documented by Tier 1 source with stated evidential basis; independently corroborated |
| **High** | Strong evidence from Tier 1–2 sources; consistent with documented patterns; no credible alternative explanation |
| **Assessed** | Credible evidence from Tier 2–3 sources; alternative explanations not ruled out; published with explicit caveat |
| **Possible** | Preliminary indicators only; single-source or uncorroborated; published where strategically significant |

Confidence is not upgraded without new evidence. Downgraded if rebuttal evidence emerges or a source withdraws or qualifies.

## 06 · Severity Ratings

Active schemes and structural findings are rated for systemic significance:

| Rating | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **CRITICAL** | Systemic sanctions-evasion architecture sustaining a conflict or weapons programme; state-level complicity documented; multi-jurisdictional enabling infrastructure |
| **HIGH** | Large-scale illicit flow with documented strategic consequences; professional-enabler network identified; regulatory enforcement gap confirmed |
| **ELEVATED** | Significant illicit-finance scheme documented but strategic consequences not yet fully assessed; architecture partially mapped |
| **MONITORED** | Credible indicators of illicit flow or enabler-jurisdiction risk; under investigation or assessment |

Severity reflects systemic significance, not headline prominence. A quiet regulatory gap enabling billions in trade-based money laundering is higher severity than a high-profile but contained enforcement action.

## 07 · Bias Corrections

The monitor applies explicit corrections to known structural biases in financial-integrity coverage:

**Enforcement-action bias.** Regulatory enforcement generates coverage; regulatory gaps do not. The Enabler Jurisdiction Filter (F3) actively searches for non-enforcement — jurisdictions where the absence of action is the signal.

**Russia overweight.** Russian sanctions evasion is the best-documented case. Iranian, DPRK, and non-state evasion architectures receive proportionate coverage regardless of media attention.

**Banking-sector focus.** Banks are visible because they are regulated. Professional enablers (lawyers, accountants, corporate service providers), real-estate channels, and trade-based money laundering carry larger volumes and lower visibility. Dedicated coverage required every cycle.

**Regional under-coverage.** Sub-Saharan Africa (extractive-industry corruption, conflict minerals), Central Asia (TBML corridors), and the Pacific (regulatory-gap exploitation) are systematically under-covered relative to their significance.

## 08 · What This Monitor Is Not

- **Not a compliance product.** The FIM produces strategic intelligence on illicit-finance architecture, not compliance screening or advisory services
- **Not an AML tool.** It does not perform transaction monitoring, customer due diligence, or regulatory reporting
- **Not a financial-crime news aggregator.** Individual cases are covered only where they reveal structural architecture
- **Not based on classified or proprietary data.** All sourcing is open-source; FIU data and law-enforcement intelligence are not available
- **Not advisory.** Findings are intelligence, not legal or regulatory advice

## 09 · Output Format

Each weekly edition produces:

1. **Global Integrity Snapshot** — active jurisdictions tracked, enforcement actions this week, architecture findings, highest-risk jurisdiction, and the lead signal
2. **Lead Signal** — the single most analytically significant development this week, assessed for architectural vs. incident significance
3. **Domain Tracker** — D1–D5 domain assessments with trajectory, key developments, and jurisdiction-level findings
4. **Jurisdiction Risk Tracker** — risk direction and basis for tracked jurisdictions, with enforcement-vs-enablement assessment
5. **Standing Tracker Synthesis** — updates to the five standing trackers where material evidence warrants
6. **Tooling Outputs** — jurisdiction risk movements, active scheme inventory, enforcement gap signals, regulatory horizon, and cross-pillar flags
7. **Key Judgments** — analytical judgments with confidence labels, domain assignment, and trajectory assessment
8. **Cross-Monitor Signals** — findings relevant to companion monitors in the Asymmetric Intelligence suite

## 11 · Limitations

1. **OSINT-only.** FIU suspicious-transaction reports, classified sanctions-enforcement intelligence, and law-enforcement operational data are not available
2. **Beneficial-ownership verification gap.** Even where registries exist, registered data accuracy is unverifiable without enforcement access
3. **TBML opacity.** Trade-based money laundering is the least visible major channel, relying on customs-data anomalies, investigative journalism, and academic estimates
4. **Professional-privilege barrier.** Legal professional privilege restricts visibility into the role of lawyers and law firms
5. **Crypto-tracing limitations.** On-chain analysis provides transaction visibility but not necessarily beneficial-ownership identification
